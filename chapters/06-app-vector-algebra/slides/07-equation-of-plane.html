6.7   Equation of a Plane

Core Idea

A plane in 3D can be specified by: (i) a unit normal and distance from origin, (ii) a point and a normal, (iii) three non-collinear points, (iv) a point and two parallel vectors, (v) two coplanar lines. Each has vector and Cartesian forms.

$$\boxed{\text{Normal form: } \vec{r} \cdot \hat{d} = p}$$
$$\boxed{\text{Point-normal: } (\vec{r} - \vec{a}) \cdot \vec{n} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \vec{r} \cdot \vec{n} = \vec{a} \cdot \vec{n}}$$
$$\boxed{(\vec{r} - \vec{a}) \cdot \vec{n} = 0}$$

Visual: Plane Defined by Point + Normal

Any point $\vec{r}$ on the plane satisfies $(\vec{r}-\vec{a})\cdot\vec{n}=0$ because $\vec{r}-\vec{a}$ lies in the plane, perpendicular to $\vec{n}$.

More Forms

$$\text{Cartesian: } a(x-x_1) + b(y-y_1) + c(z-z_1) = 0$$
$$\text{Intercept form: } \frac{x}{a} + \frac{y}{b} + \frac{z}{c} = 1$$
$$\text{General form: } ax + by + cz + d = 0 \quad\text{(normal direction ratios: } a,b,c\text{)}$$
$$ax + by + cz + d = 0$$
$$\boxed{\text{Through 3 points: } \begin{vmatrix} x-x_1 & y-y_1 & z-z_1 \\ x_2-x_1 & y_2-y_1 & z_2-z_1 \\ x_3-x_1 & y_3-y_1 & z_3-z_1 \end{vmatrix} = 0}$$
$$\boxed{\begin{vmatrix} x\!-\!x_1 & y\!-\!y_1 & z\!-\!z_1 \\ x_2\!-\!x_1 & y_2\!-\!y_1 & z_2\!-\!z_1 \\ x_3\!-\!x_1 & y_3\!-\!y_1 & z_3\!-\!z_1 \end{vmatrix} = 0}$$
$$\text{Point + 2 parallel vectors: } (\vec{r}-\vec{a}) \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{c}) = 0$$

Spot the Clue

  • "Plane passing through ... and perpendicular to ..." → normal vector is given, use point-normal
  • "Plane through three points" → use the $3 \times 3$ determinant form
  • "Plane parallel to two lines/vectors" → normal = cross product of two direction vectors
  • "Distance from origin" + "direction ratios of normal" → normal form
  • "Intercepts on axes" → intercept form

Ex: Plane Through Point, Normal Given (Ex 6.41)

Point: $4\hat{i}+2\hat{j}-3\hat{k}$, Normal: $2\hat{i}-\hat{j}+\hat{k}$

Step 1: $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{n} = (4)(2)+(2)(-1)+(-3)(1) = 8-2-3 = 3$

Step 2: Vector: $\vec{r} \cdot (2\hat{i}-\hat{j}+\hat{k}) = 3$

$$\boxed{2x - y + z = 3}$$

Watch Out

$$\text{Distance from origin to}$$ $$\vec{r} \cdot \vec{n} = q \text{ is } q$$ Only true if $\vec{n}$ is a unit vector!
$$\text{Distance} = \frac{|q|}{|\vec{n}|}$$ Always divide by $|\vec{n}|$

Trap: The normal is PERPENDICULAR to the plane, not parallel to it. For 3-point form, ensure points are non-collinear first.
