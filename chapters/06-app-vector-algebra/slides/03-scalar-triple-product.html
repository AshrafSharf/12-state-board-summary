6.3   Scalar Triple Product

Core Idea

For three vectors $\vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}$, the scalar triple product $[\vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}] = (\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{c}$ is a scalar. Its absolute value equals the volume of the parallelepiped with coterminus edges $\vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}$. It is zero if and only if the three vectors are coplanar.

$$\boxed{[\vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}] = (\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{c} = \begin{vmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \\ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{vmatrix}}$$
$$\boxed{[\vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}] = \begin{vmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \\ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{vmatrix}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{Volume of parallelepiped} = |[\vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}]|}$$

Key Properties

PropertyFormula / Result
Cyclic permutation$[\vec{a},\vec{b},\vec{c}] = [\vec{b},\vec{c},\vec{a}] = [\vec{c},\vec{a},\vec{b}]$
Swap two vectors$[\vec{a},\vec{b},\vec{c}] = -[\vec{b},\vec{a},\vec{c}]$
Scalar factor$[\lambda\vec{a},\vec{b},\vec{c}] = \lambda[\vec{a},\vec{b},\vec{c}]$
Linearity$[(\vec{a}+\vec{b}),\vec{c},\vec{d}] = [\vec{a},\vec{c},\vec{d}]+[\vec{b},\vec{c},\vec{d}]$
Coplanarity test$[\vec{a},\vec{b},\vec{c}] = 0$

Spot the Clue

  • "Find the volume of a parallelepiped" with three edge vectors
  • "Show that four points are coplanar" → form three vectors from one point, show determinant = 0
  • "Find value of $m$ for coplanarity" → set the $3 \times 3$ determinant to 0

Ex: Volume of Parallelepiped (Ex 6.13)

Edges: $\vec{a}=2\hat{i}-3\hat{j}+4\hat{k}$, $\vec{b}=\hat{i}+2\hat{j}-\hat{k}$, $\vec{c}=3\hat{i}-\hat{j}+2\hat{k}$

$$[\vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}] = \begin{vmatrix} 2 & -3 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 & -1 \\ 3 & -1 & 2 \end{vmatrix}$$
$$\begin{vmatrix} 2 & -3 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 & -1 \\ 3 & -1 & 2 \end{vmatrix}$$
$$= 2(4-1) + 3(2+3) + 4(-1-6) = 6 + 15 - 28 = -7$$
$$\boxed{\text{Volume} = |-7| = 7 \text{ cubic units}}$$

Watch Out

$$(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}) \times \vec{c}$$ Meaningless! $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}$ is a scalar — can't cross a scalar with a vector
$$(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{c}$$ Correct: cross first, then dot
$$\text{Volume} = [\vec{a},\vec{b},\vec{c}]$$ Could be negative!
$$\text{Volume} = |[\vec{a},\vec{b},\vec{c}]|$$ Always take absolute value
