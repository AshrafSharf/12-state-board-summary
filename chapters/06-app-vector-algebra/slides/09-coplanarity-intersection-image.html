6.9   Coplanarity, Intersection & Image

Core Idea

Two lines are coplanar iff $(\vec{c}-\vec{a}) \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{d}) = 0$. The line of intersection of two planes has direction $\vec{n}_1 \times \vec{n}_2$. The image of a point in a plane uses a reflection formula. The meeting point of a line and a plane is found by substituting the line into the plane equation.

Key Formulas

$$\boxed{\text{Coplanarity: } (\vec{c}-\vec{a}) \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{d}) = 0 \;\text{ or }\; \begin{vmatrix} x_2-x_1 & y_2-y_1 & z_2-z_1 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \\ d_1 & d_2 & d_3 \end{vmatrix} = 0}$$
$$\boxed{(\vec{c}\!-\!\vec{a}) \cdot (\vec{b} \!\times\! \vec{d}) = 0}$$
$$\boxed{\text{Image: } \vec{v} = \vec{u} + \frac{2[p - (\vec{u} \cdot \vec{n})]}{|\vec{n}|^2}\vec{n}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{Line-plane intersection: } \vec{u} = \vec{a} + \left(\frac{p - \vec{a} \cdot \vec{n}}{\vec{b} \cdot \vec{n}}\right)\vec{b}, \quad \vec{b} \cdot \vec{n} \neq 0}$$
$$\boxed{\vec{u} = \vec{a} + \frac{p - \vec{a} \cdot \vec{n}}{\vec{b} \cdot \vec{n}}\,\vec{b}}$$

Visual: Image (Reflection) of a Point in a Plane

The foot $F$ is the perpendicular projection. The image $P'$ is at equal distance on the other side: $\vec{v} = \vec{u} + \frac{2[p - \vec{u}\cdot\vec{n}]}{|\vec{n}|^2}\vec{n}$

More Formulas

$$\text{Plane of coplanar lines: } (\vec{r}-\vec{a}) \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{d}) = 0$$
$$\text{Direction of intersection line: } \vec{n}_1 \times \vec{n}_2$$
$$\text{Family of planes: } (\vec{r} \cdot \vec{n}_1 - d_1) + \lambda(\vec{r} \cdot \vec{n}_2 - d_2) = 0$$
$$(\vec{r}\!\cdot\!\vec{n}_1 \!-\! d_1) + \lambda(\vec{r}\!\cdot\!\vec{n}_2 \!-\! d_2) = 0$$
$$\text{Foot of perpendicular: } \vec{r}_1 = \vec{u} + \frac{p - \vec{u} \cdot \vec{n}}{|\vec{n}|^2}\vec{n}$$

Spot the Clue

  • "Show two lines are coplanar" → compute the $3 \times 3$ determinant
  • "Plane containing two lines" → $(\vec{r}-\vec{a}) \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{d}) = 0$
  • "Plane through intersection of two planes" → use $\lambda$-family, apply extra condition to find $\lambda$
  • "Image of a point in a plane" or "foot of perpendicular" → reflection formula
  • "Find where the line meets the plane" → substitute parametric line into plane equation

Ex: Image of a Point (Ex 6.55)

Point: $\hat{i}+2\hat{j}+3\hat{k}$, Plane: $\vec{r} \cdot (\hat{i}+2\hat{j}+4\hat{k}) = 38$

Step 1: $\vec{u} \cdot \vec{n} = 1+4+12 = 17$

Step 2: $|\vec{n}|^2 = 1+4+16 = 21$

$$\vec{v} = \vec{u} + \frac{2(38-17)}{21}\vec{n} = \vec{u} + 2\vec{n}$$
$$\boxed{\vec{v} = 3\hat{i}+6\hat{j}+11\hat{k}}$$

Ex: Line Meets Plane (Ex 6.56)

Line: $\vec{r} = (2\hat{i}-\hat{j}+2\hat{k})+t(3\hat{i}+4\hat{j}+2\hat{k})$
Plane: $x-y+z-5 = 0$

Step 1: $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{n} = 2+1+2 = 5$

Step 2: $\vec{b} \cdot \vec{n} = 3-4+2 = 1$

Step 3: $t = \frac{5-5}{1} = 0$

$$\boxed{(2,-1,2)}$$

Ex: Distance from Point to Intersection (Ex 6.50)

Find distance from $(5,-5,-10)$ to where line through $A(4,1,2)$, $B(7,5,4)$ meets $x-y+z=5$.

Step 1: Direction $(3,4,2)$. Parametric point: $(3t+4,\;4t+1,\;2t+2)$.

Step 2: Substitute: $(3t+4)-(4t+1)+(2t+2) = 5 \Rightarrow t = 0$. Point: $(4,1,2)$.

$$\boxed{d = \sqrt{1+36+144} = \sqrt{181} \text{ units}}$$

Watch Out

$$\vec{b} \cdot \vec{n} = 0 \;\text{and divide}$$ If $\vec{b} \cdot \vec{n} = 0$, line is parallel — no intersection!
$$\text{Check } \vec{b} \cdot \vec{n} \neq 0 \text{ first}$$ If $= 0$ and $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{n} = p$: line lies in plane

Trap: A line lies IN a plane only if BOTH $\vec{b} \cdot \vec{n} = 0$ AND the point satisfies the plane. Checking only one condition is insufficient.
