9.4   Bernoulli's Formula — Fast Integration by Parts

Spot the Clue

The integrand is a polynomial $u(x)$ times a function $v(x)$ that can be integrated repeatedly (sin, cos, $e^{ax}$).

$$\int \underbrace{u(x)}_{\text{polynomial}}\!\cdot\!\underbrace{v(x)}_{\substack{\text{can integrate} \\ \text{repeatedly}}}\,dx$$

LIATE Rule: How to Choose $u$ in Integration by Parts

When you have a product of two functions and u-sub doesn't work, use IBP. Choose $u$ as the function that simplifies when differentiated. Priority order:

$$\boxed{\underset{\text{Log}}{\mathbf{L}} \;\succ\; \underset{\text{Inverse trig}}{\mathbf{I}} \;\succ\; \underset{\text{Algebraic (poly)}}{\mathbf{A}} \;\succ\; \underset{\text{Trig}}{\mathbf{T}} \;\succ\; \underset{\text{Exponential}}{\mathbf{E}}}$$

Higher on list = choose as $u$. Everything remaining becomes $dv$.

$$\int \underbrace{x^2}_{\mathbf{A}\to u}\!\cdot\!\underbrace{e^x}_{\mathbf{E}\to dv}\,dx$$ A before E → $u=x^2$, $dv=e^x\,dx$
$$\int \underbrace{\ln x}_{\mathbf{L}\to u}\!\cdot\!\underbrace{x^2}_{\mathbf{A}\to dv}\,dx$$ L before A → $u=\ln x$, $dv=x^2\,dx$

The Formula

Let $u^{(k)}$ = $k$th derivative of $u$, and $v_{(k)}$ = $k$th integral of $v$:

$$\boxed{\int uv\,dx = u\,v_{(1)} - u^{(1)}v_{(2)} + u^{(2)}v_{(3)} - u^{(3)}v_{(4)} + \cdots}$$

Signs alternate: $+, -, +, -, \ldots$   Terminates because $u$ is a polynomial — eventually $u^{(k)}=0$.

How to Apply: The Two-Column Method

Write derivatives of $u$ going down, integrals of $v$ going down. Multiply diagonally with alternating signs.

Differentiate $u$:

$u \to u' \to u'' \to \cdots \to 0$

Integrate $v$:

$v \to v_{(1)} \to v_{(2)} \to \cdots$

Ex: $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi} x^2\cos nx\,dx$

$u=x^2$, $v=\cos nx$

$$= \left[x^2\!\cdot\!\frac{\sin nx}{n} - 2x\!\cdot\!\left(-\frac{\cos nx}{n^2}\right) + 2\!\cdot\!\left(-\frac{\sin nx}{n^3}\right)\right]_0^{\pi}$$
$$= \frac{2\pi(-1)^n}{n^2}$$

Ex: $\displaystyle\int_0^1 e^{-2x}(1+x-2x^3)\,dx$

$u=1+x-2x^3$, $v=e^{-2x}$

$$u' = 1-6x^2,\; u''=-12x,\; u'''=-12,\; u^{(4)}=0$$

Each integral of $e^{-2x}$ just divides by $-2$. Terminates in 4 terms.

Bernoulli Fails When...

Neither factor is a polynomial — derivatives never terminate.

$$\int e^x \sin x\,dx$$ Both $e^x$ and $\sin x$ cycle forever. Use standard IBP (twice + solve for $I$).
$$\int \log x\cdot\sin x\,dx$$ $\log x$ is not a polynomial. Derivatives don't terminate.
