9.2   Definite Integral as Limit of a Sum

The Big Idea

Chop $[a,b]$ into $n$ equal strips of width $h = \dfrac{b-a}{n}$. Sum up rectangle areas. Let $n\to\infty$.

$$\boxed{\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{b-a}{n}\sum_{r=1}^{n}f\!\left(a+(b-a)\frac{r}{n}\right)}$$

Special Case: $a=0,\;b=1$

$$\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{r=1}^{n}f\!\left(\frac{r}{n}\right)$$

Most textbook problems use this form.

Three Approximation Rules

  • Left-end: use $f(x_{i-1})$
  • Right-end: use $f(x_i)$
  • Mid-point: use $f\!\left(\frac{x_{i-1}+x_i}{2}\right)$

All three converge to same value as $n\to\infty$

Tip: Recognizing a Limit-of-Sum Problem

If you see $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum f\!\left(\frac{r}{n}\right)$, it is asking you to convert to $\int_0^1$.

Replace $\frac{r}{n}\to x$ and $\frac{1}{n}\to dx$.

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{r=1}^{n}\frac{r}{n} = \int_0^1 x\,dx = \frac{1}{2}$$

Key Summation Formulae You'll Need

$$\sum_{r=1}^{n}r = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}, \quad \sum_{r=1}^{n}r^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}, \quad \sum_{r=1}^{n}r^3 = \left[\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right]^2$$
$$\begin{aligned} &\textstyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}r = \frac{n(n\!+\!1)}{2} \\[4pt] &\textstyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}r^2 = \frac{n(n\!+\!1)(2n\!+\!1)}{6} \\[4pt] &\textstyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}r^3 = \left[\frac{n(n\!+\!1)}{2}\right]^2 \end{aligned}$$
