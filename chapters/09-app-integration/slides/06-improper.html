9.5 & 9.7   Improper Integrals & Gamma Function

What Makes an Integral "Improper"?

At least one limit is $\pm\infty$, or the integrand blows up somewhere in $[a,b]$.

$$\int_a^{\infty}f(x)\,dx = \lim_{t\to\infty}\int_a^t f(x)\,dx \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{b}f(x)\,dx = \lim_{t\to-\infty}\int_t^b f(x)\,dx$$

If the limit exists (is finite) → convergent. Otherwise → divergent.

Key Result: $\displaystyle\int_b^{\infty}\frac{dx}{a^2+x^2}$

$$\int_b^{\infty}\frac{dx}{a^2+x^2} = \left[\frac{1}{a}\tan^{-1}\frac{x}{a}\right]_b^{\infty} = \frac{1}{a}\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\tan^{-1}\frac{b}{a}\right)$$

Special case $b=0$: $\displaystyle\int_0^{\infty}\frac{dx}{a^2+x^2} = \frac{\pi}{2a}$  |  $b=a$: $\displaystyle\int_a^{\infty}\frac{dx}{a^2+x^2}=\frac{\pi}{4a}$

The Gamma Integral $\Gamma(n)$

$$\boxed{\Gamma(n) = \int_0^{\infty}e^{-x}x^{n-1}\,dx = (n-1)!\;,\quad n=1,2,3,\ldots}$$

Key recurrence: $\Gamma(n+1) = n\,\Gamma(n)$

Special values: $\Gamma(1)=1$,   $\Gamma(2)=1$,   $\Gamma(3)=2$,   $\Gamma(4)=6$

Generalization: $\displaystyle\int_0^{\infty}e^{-ax}x^n\,dx = \frac{n!}{a^{n+1}}$

Spot the clue: $e^{-(\text{something})x}\cdot x^n$ from $0$ to $\infty$. Substitute $t=ax$ to reduce to standard gamma.

$$\int_0^{\infty}e^{-3x}x^2\,dx = \frac{2!}{3^3}=\frac{2}{27}$$ $a=3, n=2$
$$\Gamma(n) = 2\int_0^{\infty}e^{-x^2}x^{2n-1}\,dx$$ Substitute $x=\sqrt{u}$ to prove

Convergence Check — Not All Improper Integrals Converge!

$$\int_1^{\infty}\frac{dx}{x^2} = 1 \;\checkmark$$ $\frac{1}{x^p}$ converges for $p>1$
$$\int_1^{\infty}\frac{dx}{x} = \infty \;\boldsymbol{\times}$$ $\frac{1}{x^p}$ diverges for $p\leq 1$
