9.3   Fundamental Theorems of Integral Calculus

First FTC (Differentiation undoes Integration)

If $F(x) = \displaystyle\int_a^x f(u)\,du$, then

$$\boxed{\frac{d}{dx}F(x) = f(x)}$$

The derivative of a cumulative integral gives back the original function.

Second FTC (Evaluation Formula)

If $F(x)$ is any anti-derivative of $f(x)$, then

$$\boxed{\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a) = \Big[F(x)\Big]_a^b}$$

No need for Riemann sums! Just find the anti-derivative and plug in limits.

Why "+C" Disappears in Definite Integrals

$$[F(x)+C]_a^b = \big(F(b)+C\big) - \big(F(a)+C\big) = F(b)-F(a)$$

The constant cancels. So for definite integrals, never write $+C$.

Ex: $\displaystyle\int_0^3(3x^2-4x+5)\,dx$

$$= \Big[x^3-2x^2+5x\Big]_0^3 = (27-18+15)-0 = 24$$

Ex: $\displaystyle\int_0^1\dfrac{2x+7}{5x^2+9}\,dx$

$$= \tfrac{1}{5}\log[5x^2\!+\!9]_0^1 + \tfrac{7}{5}\!\cdot\!\tfrac{1}{3}\tan^{-1}\!\tfrac{x}{3/\sqrt{5}}\Big|_0^1$$

Split into $\frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$ part + $\frac{1}{a^2+x^2}$ part
