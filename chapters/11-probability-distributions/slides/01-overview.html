Chapter 11   Probability Distributions — Overview

What This Chapter Covers

Random variables map sample-space outcomes to real numbers, creating probability distributions (discrete and continuous). The exam tests your ability to construct PMFs and CDFs, convert between them, compute mean & variance, and apply the Bernoulli and Binomial models.

Discrete World

  • Random Variables — defining $X : S \to \mathbb{R}$
  • PMF — $f(x) = P(X = x)$, with $\sum f(x) = 1$
  • CDF — $F(x) = P(X \le x)$, staircase function
  • Bernoulli — single trial, two outcomes
  • Binomial — $n$ independent trials, $X \sim B(n,p)$

Continuous World

  • PDF — $f(x) \ge 0$, $\int f(x)\,dx = 1$
  • CDF — $F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t)\,dt$
  • PDF ↔ CDF — integrate or differentiate
  • $P(X = a) = 0$ always — equality signs don’t matter

Mean & Variance — The Common Thread

Every distribution has a mean $\mu = E(X)$ and variance $\sigma^2 = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2$. Special formulas exist for Bernoulli ($\mu = p$, $\sigma^2 = pq$) and Binomial ($\mu = np$, $\sigma^2 = npq$).

Counting Principles

Throughout this chapter, counting techniques ($\binom{n}{r}$, $n^r$, $2^n$) are used to determine sample space sizes and the number of favorable outcomes. A dedicated slide covers when to use each formula.
