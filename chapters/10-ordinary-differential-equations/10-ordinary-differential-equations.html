10.1‚Äì10.2   What is a DE? Order & Degree

Definition

A differential equation contains at least one derivative of an unknown function.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = ky \quad \longleftarrow \text{ simplest DE}$$

Order & Degree

$$\underbrace{\left(\frac{d^{\color{#d97706}{4}}y}{dx^4}\right)^{\color{#dc2626}{3}}}_{\substack{\text{order}={\color{#d97706}4} \\ \text{degree}={\color{#dc2626}3}}} + 4\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^{7} + 6y = 5\cos 3x$$
  • Order = highest derivative present 4
  • Degree = power of that highest-order derivative 3

When is Degree NOT Defined?

If the equation cannot be expressed as a polynomial in its derivatives ‚Äî i.e. any derivative appears inside a transcendental function (sin, cos, log, exp).

$$\underbrace{\sin\!\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)}_{\text{derivative in sin}} + \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 3x = 0$$ Order 2, Degree: N/A
$$e^{\overbrace{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}}^{\text{derivative in exp}}} + \sin(x)\frac{dy}{dx} = 2$$ Order 2, Degree: N/A

Trick: Clear Fractional Powers First

$$\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^{\!2}} = y\frac{d^3y}{dx^3} \;\;\xrightarrow{\text{square}}\;\; 1+\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^{\!2} = y^2\!\left(\frac{d^3y}{dx^3}\right)^{\!2}$$

Now: order = 3, degree = 2

10.3   Classification of DEs

Differential Equations
ODE Ordinary derivatives only, 1 independent variable
$$\frac{dy}{dx}+2y=e^{-x}$$
PDE Partial derivatives, 2+ independent variables
$$\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}=0$$

Linear ODE

$$\overbrace{a_n(x)\,y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_1(x)\,y' + a_0(x)\,y}^{\text{no products of } y,\,y',\,y''\ldots} = g(x)$$
  • $y$ and derivatives appear to 1st power only
  • No $yy'$, $(y')^2$, $\sin y$, $e^{y'}$
  • Coefficients can be any function of $x$

Nonlinear ODE

Anything that breaks linearity:

$$y\frac{dy}{dx}+\sin x=0$$product $y \cdot y'$
$$(y')^2+y'=\sqrt{x}$$$(y')^2$ term

Homogeneous ($g(x)=0$)

Every term involves $y$ or its derivatives. Nothing "standalone" in $x$.

$$y''+2y'+y=0$$

If $y$ is a solution, so is $cy$ for any constant $c$:

$$(cy)''+2(cy)'+(cy) = c\underbrace{(y''+2y'+y)}_{=\,0}=0\;\checkmark$$

Non-homogeneous ($g(x)\neq 0$)

Has a term that is purely a function of $x$ ‚Äî not attached to $y$.

$$y''+2y'+y = \underbrace{e^{-x}}_{\text{no } y \text{ here}}$$

"But can't I move $e^{-x}$ to LHS and call it 0 on RHS?"

$$y''+2y'+y\underbrace{{}-e^{-x}}_{\text{not a term in }y}=0$$

No ‚Äî that $-e^{-x}$ doesn't involve $y$ at all. The scaling test fails:

$$(2y)''+2(2y)'+(2y)=2e^{-x}\neq e^{-x}\;\boldsymbol{\times}$$

If $y$ is a solution, $2y$ is NOT. That's what non-homogeneous means.

10.4   Formation of DEs

Why $n$ Constants Need $n$ Differentiations

To eliminate $n$ unknowns, you need $n+1$ equations (basic algebra). You start with 1 (the original). Each differentiation gives 1 more. So:

$$\underbrace{1}_{\text{original}} + \underbrace{n}_{\text{differentiations}} = n{+}1 \text{ equations with } n \text{ unknowns} \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \text{eliminate all constants}$$

The result must contain an $n$th derivative (from the $n$th differentiation), so the DE has order $n$.

Worked: $y = a\cos x + b\sin x$ (2 constants ‚Üí need 2 differentiations)

$$\underbrace{y = a\cos x + b\sin x}_{\text{eq }(1):\;\text{original ‚Äî has }a,b}$$
$$\underbrace{y' = -a\sin x + b\cos x}_{\text{eq }(2):\;\text{1st differentiation}}$$
$$\underbrace{y'' = -a\cos x - b\sin x}_{\text{eq }(3):\;\text{2nd differentiation}}$$

Now 3 equations, 2 unknowns. Notice eq$(3) = -$eq$(1)$, so:

$$y'' = -y \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \boxed{y''+y=0} \quad \text{(order 2, both } a,b \text{ gone)}$$

Lines through origin (1 const)

$$y=\underbrace{m}_{1\text{ const}}\!x \;\xrightarrow{\text{diff once}}\; y'=m$$

2 eqs, 1 unknown: substitute $m=y'$ into original:

$$\boxed{y=x\,y'}$$

$y = c_1 e^x + c_2 e^{-x}$ (2 const)

$$y'' = c_1 e^x + c_2 e^{-x} = y$$

eq(3) = eq(1) directly:

$$\boxed{y''-y=0}$$

Parabolas $y^2=4ax$

$$2yy'=4a \;\Rightarrow\; a=\frac{yy'}{2} \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{y}{2x}}$$

10.5   General vs Particular Solution

General Solution

$$\frac{dy}{dx}-2y=0 \;\Rightarrow\; y = \underbrace{c}_{\text{arbitrary}}e^{2x}$$

Contains as many arbitrary constants as the order.

Particular Solution

$$y(0)=3 \;\Rightarrow\; c=3 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 3e^{2x}$$

Substitute initial conditions to fix constants.

10.6.1   Variables Separable Method

Spot the Clue

Every term is a product of a pure-$x$ piece and a pure-$y$ piece. You can divide to isolate them.

$$\underbrace{f_1(x)}_{\text{only }x}\!\cdot\!\underbrace{g_1(y)}_{\text{only }y}\,dx \;+\; \underbrace{f_2(x)}_{\text{only }x}\!\cdot\!\underbrace{g_2(y)}_{\text{only }y}\,dy = 0$$
‚Üì divide by $f_2(x)\,g_1(y)$
$$\overbrace{\frac{f_1(x)}{f_2(x)}\,dx}^{\text{pure } x} = \overbrace{-\frac{g_2(y)}{g_1(y)}\,dy}^{\text{pure } y}$$
‚Üì integrate both sides
$$\boxed{\int \frac{f_1(x)}{f_2(x)}\,dx = -\int \frac{g_2(y)}{g_1(y)}\,dy + c}$$

The Key Test: Can You Factor?

$$\underbrace{(1+x^2)}_{\text{only }x}\frac{dy}{dx} = \underbrace{(1+y^2)}_{\text{only }y}$$ Each side is a single-variable function. Separable!
$$\underbrace{x^2}_{\text{only }x}\!\cdot\!\underbrace{y}_{\text{only }y}\,dx + \underbrace{(1+x^3)}_{\text{only }x}\,dy = 0$$ Each term = f(x) √ó g(y). Separable!

Ex: $(1+x^2)\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 1+y^2$

$$\frac{dy}{1+y^2}=\frac{dx}{1+x^2}$$
$$\tan^{-1}y = \tan^{-1}x + c$$
$$\boxed{y-x = a(1+xy)}$$

Ex: $(1\!+\!x^3)dy - x^2 y\,dx = 0$

$$\frac{dy}{y}=\frac{x^2}{1+x^3}\,dx$$
$$\log y = \tfrac{1}{3}\log(1\!+\!x^3)+\log c$$
$$\boxed{y^3 = c(1+x^3)}$$

$y(1)=2 \Rightarrow c=4$

Can't Separate When...

$x$ and $y$ are added inside a term ‚Äî no way to factor into $f(x)\cdot g(y)$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \underbrace{x + y}_{\text{sum, not product}}$$ Can't write as $h(x)\cdot k(y)$. ‚Üí Use Linear ($y'+(-1)y = x$)
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \underbrace{x^2 + xy + y^2}_{\text{mixed terms}}$$ $xy$ term couples $x,y$. ‚Üí Try Homogeneous ($y=vx$)

10.6.2   Substitution Method

Spot the Clue

The RHS is a function of one single linear combination $ax+by+c$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = f\!\Big(\;\underbrace{ax+by+c}_{\substack{\text{x and y appear ONLY} \\ \text{inside this combo}}}\;\Big)$$

Put $z = ax + by + c \;\Rightarrow\; \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{1}{b}\!\left(\dfrac{dz}{dx}-a\right)$. Now it's separable in $z, x$.

The Key Test: Is Everything Inside One $(ax+by+c)$?

$$y' = \sin^2\!\underbrace{(x-y+1)}_{\text{one combo}}$$ $a\!=\!1, b\!=\!{-1}, c\!=\!1$. Substitution!
$$y' = \underbrace{(3x+y+4)}_{a=3,\;b=1,\;c=4}\!^{2}$$ All of $x,y$ inside one combo. Substitution!

$y' = \sin^2(x-y+1)$

$$z=x\!-\!y\!+\!1,\;\frac{dz}{dx}=\cos^2 z$$
$$\boxed{\tan(x\!-\!y\!+\!1)=x+c}$$

$y' = (3x+y+4)^2$

$$z=3x\!+\!y\!+\!4,\; z'=z^2\!+\!3$$
$$\boxed{\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\tan^{-1}\!\!\left(\frac{3x+y+4}{\sqrt{3}}\right)=x+c}$$

Can't Use Substitution When...

$x$ and $y$ appear separately (not locked inside one combo), or in a ratio.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\overbrace{x+y}^{\text{combo}}}{\underbrace{x-y}_{\text{different combo}}}$$ Two different combos in a ratio. ‚Üí Homogeneous ($y=vx$)
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \underbrace{x^2}_{\text{alone}} + \underbrace{\sin(x+y)}_{\text{combo}}$$ $x^2$ sits outside the combo. Substitution won't clean up.

10.6.3   Homogeneous DEs

Spot the Clue

$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{M(x,y)}{N(x,y)}$ where $M$ and $N$ have the same total degree in every term.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\overbrace{x^2 + xy + y^2}^{\text{every term: degree 2}}}{\underbrace{x^2 - y^2}_{\text{every term: degree 2}}} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{divide by } x^2: \quad g\!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)$$

Equivalent test: replace $x\to tx,\; y\to ty$. If all $t$'s cancel from the ratio ‚Üí homogeneous.

The $y = vx$ Substitution

$$\frac{dy}{dx}=g\!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) \;\xrightarrow{\;y=vx\;}\; \underbrace{v+x\frac{dv}{dx}}_{y'=v+xv'}=g(v)$$
‚Üì rearrange & separate
$$\boxed{\int\frac{dv}{g(v)-v}=\int\frac{dx}{x}+c} \quad\text{then replace } v=\frac{y}{x}$$

Quick Degree Check

Look at each term in numerator and denominator. Add up powers of $x$ and $y$ ‚Äî they must all match.

$$\frac{\overbrace{3y^2}^{0+2=2}-\overbrace{x^2}^{2+0=2}}{\underbrace{2xy}_{1+1=2}}$$ All terms degree 2. Homogeneous!
$$\frac{\overbrace{xy}^{1+1=2}+\overbrace{y^2}^{0+2=2}}{\underbrace{x^2}_{2+0=2}}$$ All terms degree 2. Homogeneous!

$(x^2\!-\!3y^2)dx+2xy\,dy=0$

$$y=vx:\; x\frac{dv}{dx}=\frac{v^2-1}{2v}$$
$$\boxed{y^2-x^2=kx^3}$$

$y^2+x^2\dfrac{dy}{dx}=xy\dfrac{dy}{dx}$

$$y=vx:\; x\frac{dv}{dx}=\frac{v}{v-1}$$
$$\boxed{\frac{y}{x}=\log|cy|};\; y=ke^{y/x}$$

Can't Use Homogeneous When...

Terms have mismatched degrees ‚Äî or a stray constant breaks the scaling.

$$\frac{\overbrace{x^2}^{\deg 2}+\overbrace{y}^{\deg 1}}{xy}$$ Numerator has degree 2 and degree 1. Not homogeneous. ‚Üí Linear ($y' - \frac{y}{x}= x$)
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{y}{\underbrace{x+1}_{\deg 1 + \text{const}}}$$ That $+1$ breaks scaling: $f(tx,ty)\neq t^n f(x,y)$. ‚Üí Separable

10.7   First Order Linear DEs

Spot the Clue

$y$ and $y'$ appear to 1st power only ‚Äî no $y^2$, no $yy'$, no $(y')^2$, no $\sin y$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} + \underbrace{P(x)}_{\text{coeff of }y}\!\cdot y = \underbrace{Q(x)}_{\text{RHS: only }x}$$

Divide everything by the coefficient of $y'$ first to get this standard form.

Standard Form & Master Formula

$$\text{I.F.} = e^{\int P(x)\,dx}$$
‚Üì multiply both sides by I.F.
$$\boxed{y \cdot \underbrace{e^{\int P\,dx}}_{\text{I.F.}} = \int Q \cdot \underbrace{e^{\int P\,dx}}_{\text{I.F.}}\,dx + c}$$

Shorthand: $y \times \text{I.F.} = \displaystyle\int Q \times \text{I.F.}\;dx + c$

Spotting Linear Under Disguise

Often the equation doesn't look like $y' + Py = Q$ until you divide or rearrange.

$$\underbrace{(1+x^3)\frac{dy}{dx}+6x^2 y}_{\text{doesn't look standard}} = 1+x^2$$
‚Üì divide by $(1+x^3)$
$$\frac{dy}{dx}+\underbrace{\frac{6x^2}{1+x^3}}_{P(x)}\,y = \underbrace{\frac{1+x^2}{1+x^3}}_{Q(x)} \quad\checkmark$$

Alternate: $x$ as Dependent Variable

If solving for $y$ is awkward, try swapping roles:

$$\frac{dx}{dy}+P(y)\,x=Q(y) \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \text{I.F.}=e^{\int P\,dy}$$
$$\boxed{x \cdot e^{\int P\,dy} = \int Q\,e^{\int P\,dy}\,dy + c}$$

$\dfrac{dy}{dx}+2y=e^{-x}$

$$P\!=\!2,\;\text{I.F.}\!=\!e^{2x}$$
$$ye^{2x}=e^x+c$$
$$\boxed{y=e^{-x}+ce^{-2x}}$$

$\dfrac{dy}{dx}+2y\cot x=3x^2\csc^2 x$

$$P=2\cot x,\;\text{I.F.}=\sin^2 x$$
$$\boxed{y\sin^2 x=x^3+c}$$

$(1\!+\!x^3)\dfrac{dy}{dx}+6x^2y=1\!+\!x^2$

$$P=\frac{6x^2}{1+x^3},\;\text{I.F.}=(1\!+\!x^3)^2$$

$ye^y\,dx=(y^3\!+\!2xe^y)dy$

$$\frac{dx}{dy}-\frac{2}{y}x=y^2 e^{-y}$$
$$\text{I.F.}=\frac{1}{y^2}$$
$$\boxed{x=-y^2 e^{-y}+cy^2}$$

Can't Use Linear I.F. When...

$y$ appears to a power other than 1, or $y$ and $y'$ are multiplied together.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} + \underbrace{y^2}_{\text{power 2!}} = x$$ $y^2$ breaks linearity. This is a Riccati equation ‚Äî not solvable by I.F.
$$\underbrace{y\frac{dy}{dx}}_{\text{product }y\!\cdot\!y'} + 2x = 0$$ Product $yy'$. But $y\,dy = -2x\,dx$ ‚Äî it's actually Separable!
